Angela Merkel is vying for a fourth term as Chancellor when Germany elects a new Bundestag on September 24. The FT’s time-weighted average of [seven pollsters’ latest polls](http://www.wahlrecht.de/umfragen/index.htm) of voting intention suggests that Ms Merkels’s Christian Democratic Union and its Bavarian sister party, the Christian Social Union, should comfortably remain the largest party. But under Germany’s proportional representation system this is unlikely to translate into a majority of seats in the new parliament. In the Bundestag, 299 of the seats are reserved for constituency MPs elected on a first-past-the-post basis. The remainder of the MPs come from party lists, allocated to closely approximate the distribution of the party vote in each of Germany’s 16 federal states after taking into account the directly-elected MPs. At least another 299 MPs gain their seats this way, although the number can be higher. The outgoing parliament, for example, had 631 seats. A further complication is that a party must win at least five per cent of the national vote — or win three constituencies — to be included in the seat allocation process. This complex process results in a seat distribution that is very close to the national popular vote for the included parties.
## The parties * The **Christian Democrats**, an alliance of [Angela Merkel](https://www.ft.com/topics/people/Angela_Merkel)’s centre-right CDU and its Bavarian sister party, the Christian Social Union (CSU), faces [a serious challenge from the right](https://www.ft.com/content/47969426-529a-11e7-bfb8-997009366969) for the first time in its post-war history. * The centre-left **Social Democrats** (SPD) enjoyed a surge in support after selecting enjoyed a surge in support after selecting [Martin Schulz](https://www.ft.com/topics/people/Martin_Schulz) as their [new leader](https://www.ft.com/content/3a426f44-e246-11e6-8405-9e5580d6e5fb?mhq5j=e1), but have since fallen back in the polls. * The **Left** (Linke) traces its origins to East Germany’s communists and leftwingers who split from the SPD. Its figurehead [Sahra Wagenknecht](https://www.ft.com/content/6dee5340-f9ab-11e6-9516-2d969e0d3b65) remains a polarising figure, but there is one scenario where she and her co-chairman Dietmar Bartsh could find themselves in government. * The **Green** (Grüne) party’s [support has been flagging](https://www.ft.com/content/7a82714a-15f3-11e7-b0c1-37e417ee6c76) but co-chairs Katrin Göring-Eckardt and [Cem Özdemir](https://www.ft.com/stream/6eb17739-b939-38ee-8558-93ae3892b99a) could be kingmakers in coalitions with either major party. * The liberal **Free Democratic party** (FDP) narrowly failed to reach the 5 per cent threshold in 2013, but is expected to return to the Bundestag, led by 38-year-old [Christian Lindner](https://www.ft.com/content/c23bc27a-3c72-11e7-ac89-b01cc67cfeec). * The rightwing, populist **Alternative for Germany** (AfD) looks likely to win seats at the federal level for the first time. It has sought to soften its image by selecting [Alice Weidel](https://www.ft.com/content/800bc828-357e-11e7-99bd-13beb0903fa3) atop its ticket alongside Alexander Gauland, who represents its nationalist-conservative wing. ## Possible coalitions
German coalitions are often described in terms of the party colours of the participating parties. Five possible outcomes seem likely in this election: * **Grand Coalition**: Alliances of the centre-right CDU/CSU and centre-left SPD, like the current government, have become a fixture in German politics as fragmentation has made it difficult for either major party to govern with just one of the smaller parties. * **Red-Red-Green**: A left wing government led by Martin Schulz of the SPD and backed by the Left and Greens seems like the only scenario that could end Angela Merkel’s continued chancellorship. * **Black-Yellow**: The CDU/CSU parties would probably prefer a partnership with a smaller party. The free-market FDP would likely be its preferred ally; this and would be a repeat of Angela Merkel’s second term from 2009 to 2013. * **Black-Green**: A coalition between CDU/CSU and the Green party governs two of Germany’s states but has never led the Federal government. * **‘Jamaica’**: A three-way coalition with the colours of the Jamaican flag — CDU/CSU, FDP and Greens — has formed state governments including one formed in June following elections in Schleswig-Holstein. But it would be a novelty at the federal level. ## Methodology At each point in time, the FT poll-of-polls includes the most recent voting-intention poll from seven pollsters, compiled by Wahlrecht.de. The average of their results is weighted to give more recent polls greater influence on the net score.
