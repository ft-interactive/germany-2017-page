About half the Bundestag are constituency MPs elected by first-past-the-post. The remainder come from the party lists, allocated to closely approximate the distribution of the party vote after taking into account the directly-elected MPs. This results in a seat distribution that is relatively close to the national popular vote. One complication is that a party must win five per cent of the national vote — or win three constituency pluralities — for its party list votes to be included in the seat allocation process. ## The parties * [Angela Merkel](https://www.ft.com/topics/people/Angela_Merkel)’s centre-right Christian Democratic Union (and its Bavarian sister party, the Christian Social Union) * The centre-left Social Democrats (SPD) enjoyed a surge in support after selecting [Martin Schulz](https://www.ft.com/topics/people/Martin_Schulz) as their [new leader](https://www.ft.com/content/3a426f44-e246-11e6-8405-9e5580d6e5fb?mhq5j=e1), but have since fallen back in the polls. * The Left (Linke) party traces its origins to East Germany’s communists and leftwingers who split from the SPD. Its figurehead [Sahra Wagenknecht](https://www.ft.com/content/6dee5340-f9ab-11e6-9516-2d969e0d3b65) remains a polarising figure, but there is one scenario where she and her co-chairman Dietmar Bartsh could find themselves in government. * The [Green](https://www.ft.com/stream/66be4f3c-4c22-3bea-91cb-85cc3f504d0b) (Grüne) party’s [support has been flagging](https://www.ft.com/content/7a82714a-15f3-11e7-b0c1-37e417ee6c76) but co-chairs Katrin Göring-Eckardt and [Cem Özdemir](https://www.ft.com/stream/6eb17739-b939-38ee-8558-93ae3892b99a) could be kingmakers in coalitions with either major party. * The liberal [Free Democratic party](https://www.ft.com/stream/5155b480-7d86-3d92-b364-9591051447ea) (FDP) was booted out of the Bundestag after failing to reach the 5 per cent threashold in 2013, but is expected to return, led by 38-year-old [Christian Lindner](https://www.ft.com/content/c23bc27a-3c72-11e7-ac89-b01cc67cfeec). * The rightwing populist [Alternative for Germany](https://www.ft.com/stream/77210aa2-bfe4-3190-b76f-1603ab1a8b16) (AfD) looks likely to win seats at the federal level for the first time. It has sought to soften its image by selecting [Alice Weidel](https://www.ft.com/content/800bc828-357e-11e7-99bd-13beb0903fa3), who is gay, atop its ticket alongside Alexander Gauland, who represents its nationalist-conservative wing. ## Possible coalitions German coalitions are often described in terms of the party colours of the parties of which they are comprised. Five possible outcomes seem likely in this election: * **Grand Coalition**: Alliances of the centre-right CDU and centre-left SPD, like the current government, have become a fixture in German politics as fragmentation has made it difficult for either major party to govern with just one of the smaller parties. * **Red-Red-Green**: An leftwing government led by Martin Schulz of the SPD and backed by the Left and Greens, seems like the only scenario that could end Angela Merkel’s continued Chancellorship. * **Black-Yellow**: The CDU would probably prefer a partnership with a smaller party, and the free-market FDP would likely be its preferred ally. * **Black-Green**: A coalition between the CDU and the Green party currenty governs two of Germany's states, but has never run the Federal government. * **Jamaica**: A three-way coalition with the colours of the Jamaican flag — CDU, FDP and Greens — has formed state governments including one formed in June following elections in Schleswig-Holstein. But it would be a novelty at the federal level. ## Methodology At each point in time, the FT poll-of-polls includes the most recent voting-intention poll from seven pollsters, compiled by Wahlrecht.de. The average of their results is weighted to give more recent polls greater influence on the net score.
